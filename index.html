Introduction to Magic: The Gathering

Alright, you've decided that you're interested in learning Magic, but are unsure how to play and how to parse the cards that you see. Don't worry - this guide is here to get you started with that. It's by no means comprehensive, as anyone who plays will tell you that it can get complicated, and there's always more to learn about how it works. This will be a primer to get you playing some quick games, and then you'll be on your way to learning the rest as you go. Keep in mind, that for almost everything that I say here, there are cards and effects that make an exception. But it's best to learn the base behavior before getting into all the exceptions. I'll broadly break this primer into six main subjects:

  1. The Card Types and Card Structure
  2. The Board
  3. The Turn Structure
  4. Jargon
  5. The Colors

Unfortunately for this guide, because of the nature of the game, there are plenty of important elements that require an understanding of multiple of these parts, so I don't think I'll be able to talk about them fully in isolation. Hopefully this order of subjects helps with that.

Card Types and Card Structure

Let's start by looking at a few cards to get familiar with their basic structure.
See: Braids, Conjurer Adept
We can see two horizontal ribbons of brief text, on along the top edge of the card, and one about halfway down. There's some art in between them, and a bunch of text in the bottom half.
The left side of the top ribbon has the card's name - this is unique for this card, meaning every card with the same name is identical in every mechanical sense, though sometimes the art or styling might be a little different. The mana cost of the card is shown in the card's top right - this represents how much of each kind of mana (the main resource in the game) you'll need to cast this card. We won't cover mana just yet.
Between the two ribbons is the art for the card. This isn't relevant to the mechanics of the game, but does a lot to make the game feel more immersive and thematic, and it's often just pretty.
The middle ribbon shows the type of the card, along with any card subtypes and supertypes. The main types are land, creature, artifact, enchantment, planeswalker, sorcery, instant. Anything you see before the card type is a supertype (e.g. Legendary), and anything after the card type is a subtype (e.g. Human). We won't get too much into these since they're less fundamental to the workings of the game, and are instead usually used in the mechanics of individual cards.
And lastly, in the bottom half, we see a bunch of text. This includes the mechanical abilities and effects of the card if it has any, and below that is the flavor text. The flavor text isn't mechanically important to the game, but like the art, grounds the card into the narrative and world of Magic. You'll see some great one sentence stories, hilarious puns, and glib remarks here.
Let's discuss more about the card types. You can identify the type of a card by looking at the line of text that goes horizontally through the middle of the card.

  • Land - with rare exception, these produce the mana that you'll use to play other cards and generate effects. They usually look like locations and terrain in the art.
    See: Forest
  • Creature - these are monsters, characters, animals, etc. Animated, living things that can move. These are often used to attack and deal damage to your opponent to win the game. They have a pair of numbers in the bottom right to represent their strength in combat - the first is the power: the amount of damage it can dish out, and the second is its toughness: the amount of damage needed to kill it.
    See: Abyssal Gatekeeper
  • Artifact - these are items and objects in the world of Magic. Statues, amulets, wands, swords, etc. There are plenty of different kinds, with all sorts of different effects. It's hard to put them in a clear box on their mechanics.
    See: Helvault
  • Enchantment - these mechanically behave a little like artifacts, but thematically represent more abstract things, like morale or ongoing magical effects on parts of the world.
    See: Brave the Sands
  • Planeswalker - these represent particularly powerful characters in Magic, who are almost like other players. They usually have a small collection of abilities, and they can be directly attacked by creatures in combat. These are relatively rare, but not too complicated mechanically.
    See: Garruk Wildspeaker
  • Sorcery - these are one-time effects that you can play only during your turn, often representing a sudden event or a single magical spell.
    See: Acquire
  • Instant - these are one-time effects that you can play almost any time, also usually representing a sudden event or magical spell. These can get mechanically complicated, mainly because of when you can cast them. We'll treat them basically like sorceries until you've got a better overall understanding of the game's basics.
    See: Abrade

We could get into the different workings of each card type here, but they're tightly related to the elements of a turn, so we'll go on to that and discuss them there.
Instead, lets talk a little about mana and mana costs. Mana comes in five colors - White {W}, Blue {U}, Black {B}, Red {R}, Green {G}. These are generated respectively by Plains, Island, Swamp, Mountain, and Forest lands by tapping them. You will also often see numbers in gray circles like {3} shown in the costs of things. This means three generic mana, for which the color doesn't matter. You may also see {C}, which means a colorless mana - it can't be used for any colored costs, but can be put towards generic mana costs.
For example, if you look at Braids' mana cost ({2}{U}{U}), it shows that to cast her, you need to spend two mana of any color, plus two blue mana, such as from Islands. Four in total. Abrade costs two mana - one of any color, and one red.
Once a creature or other permanent is cast and is on the board, you don't need to pay its cost again to keep it out or to attack with it. Mana costs can also show up in the abilities of cards.

Board Structure

Each player has a library (deck), a hand, and a graveyard (discard pile).
Most of play happens on the battlefield - this is where you'll play your lands, creatures, planeswalkers, artifacts, and enchantments (all are collectively called permanents). Instants and sorceries don't stick around, and so don't stay on the battlefield, they are cast and go straight to the graveyard.
Most of your cards start in your library, until they are drawn into your hand, and then cast onto the battlefield (note: there is another zone involved here, called the stack, but this is more complicated, and will be discussed later). Then, if/when they are destroyed, they go to the graveyard. Any cards discarded from your hand also go to the graveyard.
Lastly, there is exile. Especially at low level play, this isn't all that relevant, but it's kind of a catch-all place to put cards that aren't anywhere else. Cards don't really go there unless there's some kind of effect that makes it happen, and they don't usually leave exile unless the effect that put them in exile has a way for them to leave.
Each player has a maximum hand size of seven cards - which doesn't come up all that often, and is only checked at the end of that player's turn.

Start of the Game and Turn Structure

When you start the game, each player draws seven cards, and they randomly choose who will go first. In a one-on-one match, the player to go first skips their first draw for turn.
At a basic level, there are five parts to each turn before it moves to the next player's turn:

  1. The beginning phase (untap, upkeep, draw)
  2. The first main phase (can do most of your stuff here)
  3. The combat phase (try to do damage to your opponent: begin combat, declare attackers, declare blockers, combat damage, end of combat)
  4. The second main phase (can do most of your stuff here)
  5. The ending phase (end step, cleanup)

It's a little weird that there are two different sections called the main phase where you can do most of your stuff, but that's how the game is structured, and there's plenty of cards and strategy that make both relevant. Let's cover parts of a turn in order.

Starting section - untap, upkeep, draw:
During the start of your turn you first untap your permanents, meaning that you re-ready them for your turn. Creatures usually become tapped when they attack, and lands become tapped when they're used for mana. Other permanents can become tapped for any number of reasons, but those are the most universal.
Next is the upkeep step - nothing inherently happens here, but there are plenty of effects that want you to do something at the beginning of your turn, marked by words like “at the beginning of your upkeep” - those happen here.
Last is the draw step - this is pretty self-explanatory, you draw a card.

During each of the main phases is when you can play your lands (only one per turn, not one per main phase), you can cast sorceries, artifacts, enchantments, creatures, and planeswalkers. Or instants, but the main value of instants is that you can cast them at other times, like your (or others') upkeep, any time during combat, or even during someone's end step.

While there are technically five steps to the combat phase, only the middle three are really commonly relevant.

  1. Beginning combat just means that everyone agrees to move to combat, and that we're no longer in the main phase.
  2. Declare attackers - these next three are the important ones. The active player, the person whose turn it is, can choose to attack another player with any number of their creatures.
    Details - creatures that you played this turn cannot attack, they have to wait a turn to lose their summoning sickness. You won't get another opportunity to attack until your next turn, and can't change your mind after this point about who you attack or which creatures you attack with. All creatures declared as attackers become tapped. If a creature is tapped before combat, it cannot attack. You attack the player (or planeswalker), not the player's creatures. They'll choose how to block in a moment.
  3. Declare blockers - the player(s) who are being attacked, choose which creatures they want to use to block, and how they will block. Once declared, they cannot be changed.
    Details - a tapped creature (e.g. one that attacked) cannot be declared as a blocker. A given creature can block up to one attacking creature, but multiple creatures can block the same attacking creature. Many abilities of creatures restrict how they can be blocked, like Flying. Also, creatures do not need to tap to block.
  4. Combat damage - damage is applied to players, attacking creatures, and blocking creatures all at the same time. Any creature (attacking or blocking) that has taken damage greater than or equal to its toughness immediately dies and goes to the graveyard. Note, damage on a creature only lasts until the end of the turn, so if it doesn't die this turn, it sort of resets. But if it takes more damage from another effect in the same turn, it'll still die.
  5. End of Combat - everyone agrees to move out of combat into the next main phase, not super important.

The second main phase is identical to the first.

The end step is where most temporary effects end, and where you check your hand size. If you have more than seven cards in hand, discard down to seven - you choose which cards to discard.

It's probably a good idea to play through a basic game or two at this point to help solidify a basic understanding of card types, how things move among different parts of the board, and how turns work. The next sections are largely about reading and understanding more of the text on individual cards.
I want to note that a lot of the complexity that will be discussed later comes from the small waiting periods between two things happening, and from when different decisions need to be made. Such as a small window between casting and resolving a spell, timing on the upkeep, moving between phases, etc. It'll be discussed a little in The Stack, but won't be covered in detail in this primer. Just something to keep an eye out for.

Jargon

Mechanical words:

  • Permanent - Land, Creature, Artifact, Enchantment, Planeswalker. Any card that sticks around, not an instant or sorcery.
  • Tap - represented by turning the card 90 degrees sideways. Attacking creatures tap, lands are tapped to produce mana, and many abilities on permanents require them to tap.
  • Sacrifice - usually a cost for something but not always. Choosing a permanent with a type specified by the effect to go to the graveyard.
  • Dies - when a creature goes from the battlefield to the graveyard.
  • Cast - when a card has a mana cost paid and is moved onto the stack, usually from the players hand.
  • Spell - this is a little tricky: whenever a card is on the stack, it is a spell. E.g. a creature that has its cost paid but hasn't entered the battlefield yet is a creature spell. Same for an instant that has been paid for but hasn't had its effect happen yet. Note: lands are not cast, do not use the stack, and so are never spells.
  • Destroy - an effect that causes a permanent to go from the battlefield to the graveyard.
  • When/Whenever/At - if it's the first word, this signifies a triggered ability.
  • “:” - signifier for recognizing an activated ability. The cost for the ability comes before the colon, and the effect comes after
  • Exile - the exile zone, or the act of putting something into that zone. E.g. Exile target creature.
  • Mill - put a specified number of cards directly from your library into your graveyard.

  • Target -

Player terminology

  • Ramp - getting access to more mana - either by playing cards that get more lands, or artifacts/creatures/enchantments that generate mana. Also a strategy focused on getting lots of mana quickly.
  • Swing - to attack
  • Sac - shorthand for sacrifice

Colors

This section is more about flavor and feel than gameplay mechanics, but I think is still an important part to immersing yourself in this wonderfully complex game.
Magic has five colors - White (sun), Blue (water drop), Black (skull), Red (fire), and Green (tree). A card can be any number of colors, or none at all, as in the case for many artifacts.
Personally, one of my favorite things about Magic is the color pie. Each color represents a sort of philosophy or attitude towards the world, and despite appearances, no color is inherently good or inherently bad. There are plenty of great articles on this.
If we take a look at the back side of a magic card, we'll see the five colors arranged in a circle. Each of the colors (philosophies) has two “ally” colors, shown next to the color on the circle, and two “enemy” colors, shown opposite. This means that no two colors are diametrically opposed.

  • White - seeks peace through order. Could be a peaceful society, or could be totalitarianism with enforced uniformity.
  • Blue - seeks perfection through knowledge. Can be great scholars, or can be controlling manipulators.
  • Black - seeks power through selfishness. Can be a paragon of self empowerment, or a backstabbing traitor. Not inherently evil, though a lot of the writers didn't get that message.
  • Red - seeks freedom through action. Can be passionate and expressive, or can be angry and violent.
  • Green - seeks harmony through community growth. Can be deeply at peace with itself, or can be staunchly against progress and directed change.

Okay, all this philosophizing is great, but what does it mean? There aren't any hard mechanics to cover here, but the basic idea is that each color has some things that they're good at, and some things that they're bad at.

  • Green is great at getting more lands and mana, and at having powerful creatures relative to their cost, but struggles with removing enemy cards.
  • Blue is great at drawing cards, but tends to have weak creatures for the cost.
  • Black is great at removing enemy creatures, and has some access to effects from the other colors, but usually has to hurt itself while doing so.
  • Red is great at hitting quickly and explosively, but has a hard time with defense.
  • White is great at defense, but doesn't draw a lot of cards.

As mentioned before, cards can be of multiple colors. And of course, you can include cards of multiple colors in your deck too, or even all colors. This can help a lot with covering a given color's weaknesses, and can give the deck more complex strategies. It's not all upside, though - you have to have the right colors at the right time. That's a deckbuilding discussion.
